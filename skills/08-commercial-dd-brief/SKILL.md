## Classification Framework: KPMG BuildingTech Taxonomy

All assessments are positioned within the KPMG BuildingTech taxonomy, which segments construction tech along four pillars:

1. **PropTech** — Real estate and asset management technology (tenant management, lease administration, maintenance, energy, sustainability)
2. **ConTech** — Construction operations and field management (project management, field productivity, safety, equipment management, supply chain)
3. **CRETech** — Commercial Real Estate analytics and decision-support (market analysis, transaction advisory, valuation, ESG reporting)
4. **Smart Building Tech** — Building systems and IoT integration (access control, occupancy analytics, building performance)

The target company is classified within one or more of these quadrants, with competitive positioning relative to category leaders.

## Four Critical Misconceptions About Construction Tech Valuations

The report explicitly addresses these misconceptions, which mislead PE valuations:

### 1. SaaS Multiples Are Not Directly Transferable
Construction tech companies are often valued on SaaS multiples (10–20× ARR for growth SaaS). However:
- **Construction tech CAC is 2–3× higher** than enterprise SaaS due to sales-driven adoption
- **Churn rates are 20–30% annually**, vs. 5–10% for vertical SaaS
- **Sales cycles are 6–12 months**, not 3–6 months
- **Implication:** A construction tech company at $5M ARR with 25% churn and $100K CAC is worth 5–8× ARR, not 12–15× ARR

### 2. "Land Grab" Strategy Requires Longer Payback Windows
Early-mover advantage in construction tech is real but requires patient capital:
- Market adoption is slower than software markets (GCs are risk-averse, process-heavy)
- Payback on customer acquisition is 18–36 months, not 12–18 months
- Strategic value (ecosystem lock-in, data assets) may exceed immediate profitability

### 3. "Network Effect" Claims Are Often Premature
Construction tech does benefit from network/marketplace dynamics, but:
- Two-sided adoption (GCs + subs/vendors) is slower than single-side
- Data value is high but requires scale (5,000+ active projects, 50,000+ users) to monetize
- Early claims of "network defensibility" often overstate competitive moat

### 4. Consolidation/Roll-Up Thesis Requires Architectural Alignment
"Buy best-of-breed and roll up" strategies popular in PE face hidden integration costs:
- APIs and data interoperability between legacy construction systems is poor
- Field adoption requires consistent UX; fragmented tool stacks reduce user engagement
- Staff retention post-acquisition is critical (domain talent is scarce)

## Six Due Diligence Components

### Component 1: Technology Assessment

**Objective:** Validate technology differentiation, maturity, and sustainability.

**Scope:**
- Architecture review: cloud-native vs. legacy; scalability and performance
- Feature gap analysis: product maturity relative to category leaders
- Roadmap credibility: product strategy aligned to market trends?
- Technical debt: architectural constraints limiting feature velocity?
- Data/IP assets: proprietary data sets, algorithms, or machine learning differentiation?

**Key Questions:**
- Is the technology defensible vs. point solutions or category incumbents?
- Are there material technical risks (scalability, security, uptime)?
- What is the cost of ongoing R&D to stay competitive?

**Output:** 2–3 page technology assessment + 1 technical risk summary

---

### Component 2: Market Validation

**Objective:** Confirm addressable market size, growth trends, and buyer concentration.

**Scope:**
- Total addressable market (TAM): construction spend by segment × software intensity
- Serviceable addressable market (SAM): target segment size
- Market growth rate: historical and projected (compare to PropTech data: $11.2B invested 2025, 45% CAGR ConTech)
- Buyer concentration: top 10 customers as % of revenue (risk metric)
- Emerging trends: labor shortages, digital adoption acceleration, ESG requirements

**Data Sources:**
- PitchBook, CB Insights, Tracxn for funding/valuation benchmarks
- CRETI (Construction Research and Economics) for market sizing
- Published analyst reports (McKinsey, Goldman Sachs, JLL, CBRE)
- Client customer interviews (if available)

**Output:** 3–4 page market assessment + TAM/SAM summary + growth chart

---

### Component 3: Competitive Positioning

**Objective:** Benchmark the target company against category competitors and adjacent solutions.

**Scope:**
- Direct competitors: same use case, same end-user
- Indirect competitors: different approach to same problem (e.g., large ERP vs. vertical best-of-breed)
- Competitive differentiation: feature, price, go-to-market
- Win/loss analysis: where does target win vs. lose?
- Pricing/unit economics: target vs. competitors

### Component 4: Team Evaluation

**Objective:** Assess management team credibility and retention risk.

**Scope:**
- Founding team backgrounds: industry experience, startup track record, domain expertise
- Key hires: talent density, domain depth, retention risk (when do key employees have vesting cliffs?)
- Organizational structure: coverage gaps, reporting lines
- Advisory board: credibility and operating support
- Management turnover: historical turnover rate, why people left

**Key Questions:**
- Do founders have domain credibility (former GC/sub/vendor operator, or software founder with domain expertise)?
- Are key domain experts (product, sales) locked in post-acquisition (equity, earnout structure)?
- Is there a capable #2/COO to support growth?

**Output:** 1–2 page team summary + key person risk assessment

---

### Component 5: Financial Model Review

**Objective:** Validate unit economics, revenue projections, and path to profitability.

**Scope:**
- Historical financials: revenue growth, gross margin, CAC payback, churn
- Assumptions audit: are projections realistic vs. benchmarks?
- Unit economics: cost per customer, customer lifetime value (LTV), LTV/CAC ratio
- Path to profitability: breakeven timing, required capital
- Sensitivity analysis: downside case (30% lower ARR growth, 30% higher churn)

**Benchmark Metrics for Construction Tech:**
- Gross margin: 70–80% (SaaS standard)
- CAC: $50K–$150K per customer (high for construction)
- CAC payback: 18–36 months (long for construction)
- Churn: 20–30% annually (high for software)
- Magic Number (ARR growth / CAC spend): 0.5–0.8× (lower than pure SaaS)

**Output:** 2–3 page financial summary + model sensitivity + key assumption validation

---

### Component 6: Risk Assessment & Recommendation

**Objective:** Synthesize findings into risk register and investment recommendation.

**Scope:**
- Technology risk: obsolescence, scalability, data security
- Market risk: adoption slowdown, competitive disruption, pricing pressure
- Team risk: key person dependencies, retention post-acquisition
- Financial risk: path to profitability, cash runway, financing needs
- Execution risk: integration complexity (if acquisition), go-to-market challenges

**Risk Register Template:**
| Risk | Probability | Impact | Mitigation | Owner |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| | | | | |

**Recommendation Options:**
- Proceed (strong fit for thesis)
- Proceed with conditions (de-risk areas identified)
- Pass (misalignment with fund thesis or material risk)

**Output:** 1–2 page risk summary + risk register

## 2–3 Page Executive Summary Format

### Structure
1. **Investment Thesis Alignment** (3–4 sentences): How does this deal fit the fund's thesis? Which construction tech segment?
2. **Company Overview** (3–4 sentences): Founded when, founders, current stage, recent funding, revenue run rate
3. **Technology & Market Position** (5–6 sentences): What does it do? Key differentiation? Market size and growth?
4. **Key Strengths** (3–4 bullets): What's most compelling?
5. **Key Risks** (3–4 bullets): What needs to be addressed?
6. **Financial Snapshot** (2–3 sentences): Revenue, growth, margin profile, path to profitability
7. **Recommendation** (2–3 sentences): Proceed? Conditions? Pass?
